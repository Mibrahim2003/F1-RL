# The Vision

## What I am building

A Formula 1 race that runs on its own. Twenty-two cars, real circuits, a full field fighting for position. The drivers are not human. Each car is driven by artificial intelligence, and each one learns to race. They learn the track, find speed, and battle the cars around them. I set the race in motion and watch what happens.

I love Formula 1. I wanted something of my own that holds the same tension and drama. I did not want a recording of a real race. I wanted a living race I built, where the outcome surprises me every time.

## What the race looks like

You watch from above, looking straight down on the circuit. The whole track fits on the screen at once, drawn faithfully so you recognize the shape of a real Grand Prix venue. The cars move across it as small, clean shapes, sized in true proportion to the track. A car looks the right size against the width of the road and the length of a straight. The scale feels honest, the way a real race looks from a helicopter camera high above.

Nothing is heavy or three-dimensional. The beauty comes from clarity. You see every car, every position, every move, all at a glance.

## The drivers

The cars teach themselves to race. At the start they are clumsy. They drift wide, brake too late, and lose the track. Over time they sharpen. They learn where to turn in, where to hold speed, and where to risk the edge. A good driver finds the fast way around, lap after lap.

Then the field comes alive. Cars defend their position. Cars hunt the car ahead and look for a way past. Some moves work and some end in contact. The race tells a different story every time, because the drivers react to each other in the moment.

## The tracks

I want the real 2026 calendar, circuit by circuit. Each track keeps the true layout, the famous corners, the long straights and tight chicanes people know by name. When the field races at one venue, the shape on screen matches the place in the world. The track is not a rough sketch. The track is the real thing, close enough for a fan to recognize the corner before the label appears.

## The details that make it real

The part I care about most is honesty. A race is more than cars going fast in a circle. Real racing comes from the choices and the limits, and I want those here.

The racing line matters. I do not want cars driving down the middle of the road every lap. I want them to use the full width, to clip the inside of a corner and run out wide on exit, to find the fast line the way real drivers do. The best path through a corner is something each driver earns.

Mistakes carry weight. A car runs slightly wide and touches the edge, and the price is small, the same as in a real race. A car overshoots the corner and slides far onto the grass or the gravel, and the price is real. Small errors stay forgiving. Large errors cost the driver. I never want every car perfectly on the road forever. Cars push, cars reach too far, and the edge catches the ones who overreach.

Tires shape the race. A soft tire grips hard and turns fast laps, then fades as the rubber wears. A hard tire gives less speed and lasts far longer. A driver trades raw pace against the life of the tire, and the right choice depends on the moment.

Weather changes everything. A dry track under sun rewards aggression. Rain drops the grip across the whole circuit and turns a confident lap into a careful one. The same corner asks for two different drives depending on the sky.

The surface plays a part too. The road, the kerbs at the edge, the runoff beyond. Each behaves differently under the tires, and a smart driver respects the difference.

Pit stops come later, and they belong in the dream. A stop costs time in the moment and pays back over the following laps, through fresh rubber and a new plan. The choice of when to stop becomes a strategy of its own. I want this layer once the racing works on its own.

## How the dream grows

The project arrives in stages, and each stage stands on its own.

First, one car learns one simple track, and finds its way around cleanly. Seeing a single car teach itself the road is the first reward.

Next, the car meets a real circuit, and laps a true Grand Prix layout.

Then one driver learns to handle every track on the calendar, not one alone.

After this, the field fills. Many cars share the road, race wheel to wheel, overtake, and defend. A real grid takes shape.

Later, the world deepens. Tires wear, the weather turns, the surface bites, and the racing line and off-track rules give the racing its honesty.

Last, pit stops and strategy arrive, and the race gains its final layer of drama.

Every step leaves something worth watching. Nothing waits until the end to feel alive.

## Why I am doing this

Three reasons, in order.

Fun comes first. I want to sit back, start a race, and feel the same pull a real Grand Prix gives me. A race I made. A race full of surprises. A race I want to watch again. This is the reason the project exists.

A place on my resume comes second. A finished build of this size says something real about what I am able to do.

Learning comes third, and learning runs through the whole project. I want to understand every piece of how this works, from the shape of a track to the moment a driver finds the perfect corner.

## What success looks like

A full grid of twenty-two cars, racing a real 2026 circuit, seen from above. Cars fight for position. A driver on worn tires defends from a driver on fresh ones. Rain arrives and reshuffles the order. Late in the race, a well-timed stop wins a place. I watch the whole thing run on its own, and I feel the same tension a real race gives me.

When I have a video of the race worth showing to anyone, the dream is real.
